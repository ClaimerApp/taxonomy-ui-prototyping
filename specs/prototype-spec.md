# Taxonomy Prototype Specification

**Updated Draft — Sensors, Interceptors, and Signals**

---

# 1. Product Purpose

Taxonomy is infrastructure for **proactive tax advisory**.

Rather than helping advisors work faster on tasks they already know about, Taxonomy continuously analyses a firm’s data ecosystem to detect **important changes, risks, opportunities, and errors**.

When something meaningful occurs, the system generates a **Signal** and alerts the appropriate advisor.

In simple terms:

> Taxonomy monitors everything that matters across a firm’s data and workflows and alerts advisors when something important has happened, is about to happen, or is happening right now.

Signals are generated by two core system components:

**Sensors** and **Interceptors**.

The application interface primarily acts as:

* a **Signal feed**
* a **drill-down investigation environment**
* a **configuration surface for Sensors and Interceptors**

Most value is delivered through **push notifications**, with the application used when deeper context or management is required.

---

# 2. Core System Concepts

## 2.1 Sensors

**Sensors** continuously monitor data sources and detect meaningful changes over time.

They operate across the firm’s entire information environment, analysing structured and unstructured data.

Examples of data monitored by Sensors:

* accounting systems
* tax filings
* emails and communications
* documents and deliverables
* regulatory updates
* public filings
* grant databases
* market signals

Sensors detect patterns, changes, or conditions that require attention.

Examples of Sensor outputs:

* eligibility for a tax relief
* legislative changes affecting a client
* deterioration in a client’s financial health
* a grant opportunity becoming relevant
* new Companies House filings
* emerging client risk indicators

When a Sensor detects something meaningful, it generates a **Signal**.

---

## 2.2 Interceptors

**Interceptors** operate inside workflows and perform **real-time checks before actions are completed**.

Where Sensors observe changes over time, Interceptors verify actions **at the moment they occur**.

Examples include checking:

* outbound client emails
* advisory deliverables
* tax filings
* calculations
* document attachments
* claims made in written advice

An Interceptor evaluates the action against the system’s knowledge graph and regulatory context.

If a potential issue is detected, the Interceptor generates a Signal before the action is finalised.

Example flow:

```
Advisor prepares client email
↓
Email Interceptor scans message
↓
Potential advisory inconsistency detected
↓
Advisor is warned before sending
```

Example message:

```
Potential advisory inconsistency detected.

You previously advised this client that R&D relief may not apply
due to subcontractor status. This email suggests eligibility.

Review before sending?
```

Interceptors are designed to **prevent errors before they reach clients**, protecting both the firm and the advisory relationship.

---

## 2.3 Signals

A **Signal** is an actionable insight generated by a Sensor or Interceptor.

Signals may represent:

* advisory opportunities
* regulatory changes affecting a client
* risk detection
* workflow errors
* external events
* operational issues
* client relationship signals

Signals always belong to an **entity**, which may be:

* a **Company**
* a **Person**

Entities may represent:

* active clients
* past clients
* prospects
* contacts discovered in communications
* organisations referenced in documents or public data

This allows Taxonomy to surface insights **even before a formal client relationship exists**.

---

# 3. Signal Categories

Signals generally fall into three categories.

## Opportunity Signals

Sensors detect opportunities for advisory work hidden within the firm’s data.

Examples:

* newly relevant tax relief
* grant eligibility
* structural optimisation opportunities
* planning opportunities

---

## Value Signals

Sensors detect changes that allow advisors to proactively inform clients about developments that affect them.

Examples:

* legislative changes affecting a client structure
* HMRC guidance updates
* industry developments
* relevant regulatory updates

These Signals strengthen the advisor’s role as a trusted strategic partner.

---

## Risk Signals

Sensors and Interceptors detect risks affecting the client, the firm, or the advisory relationship.

Examples:

* clerical errors in deliverables
* incorrect claims in communications
* filing inconsistencies
* deteriorating client financial health
* administration risk
* operational risks within the firm
* potential client churn indicators

Some risk Signals may be **mission-critical**, requiring immediate attention.

---

# 4. Signal Lifecycle

Signals progress through a lifecycle as they are handled.

```
New
Viewed
In Progress
Resolved
Dismissed
Deferred
```

Typical resolution actions may include:

* contacting the client
* correcting a document
* creating a CRM task
* updating advice
* dismissing an irrelevant signal

Signals may also be resolved **directly from notification channels**, such as email.

---

# 5. Delivery Model

Taxonomy follows a **push-first interaction model**.

Advisors receive Signals through the tools they already use.

Primary delivery channels:

* Email
* Slack or Microsoft Teams
* CRM systems
* Practice management platforms

The application acts as the **central intelligence interface**, but most Signals will be surfaced through notifications.

---

## Signal Delivery Types

### Immediate Alerts

Reserved for high-urgency Signals.

Examples:

* filing deadline risks
* serious compliance risks
* major regulatory changes affecting a client
* workflow errors detected by Interceptors

These are delivered immediately.

---

### Daily Digest

The primary delivery mechanism.

Includes Signals such as:

* advisory opportunities
* moderate risks
* external client developments

Example digest:

```
Taxonomy Signals — Tuesday

3 important signals detected

1. R&D relief opportunity
Company: Alpha Robotics
Estimated saving: £42k

2. Director loan risk detected
Company: Harper Holdings

3. Grant eligibility surfaced
Company: GreenGrid Energy
```

Signals can be viewed, dismissed, or actioned directly from the email.

---

### Weekly Summary

Lower-priority signals such as:

* industry developments
* general regulatory updates
* broader market signals

---

# 6. Application Structure

The Taxonomy application is designed as a **Signal Intelligence Interface**, not a traditional analytics dashboard.

Core functions include:

* viewing and managing Signals
* investigating evidence and sources
* exploring entity intelligence
* configuring Sensors and Interceptors

---

# 8. Signal Feed (Landing Page)

The default landing page is a **Signal Feed**.

The feed behaves similarly to an inbox.

Example layout:

```
Signals

Filters:
New | In Progress | Resolved | All

------------------------------------------------

R&D relief opportunity detected
Company: Alpha Robotics
Detected: 2 hours ago

Potential saving: £42k

[View Signal]
[Dismiss]

------------------------------------------------

Possible advisory inconsistency detected
Person: John Carter

Triggered by Email Interceptor
Detected: 20 minutes ago
```

Users can filter Signals by:

* urgency
* entity
* Sensor / Interceptor
* signal type
* status

Signals may also be grouped by:

* company
* person
* signal category

Default sorting is by **urgency**.

# 9. Signal Detail View

Opening a Signal provides deeper context.

### Signal Summary

```
R&D relief opportunity detected

Company: Alpha Robotics
Confidence: High
Detected by: R&D Sensor
Potential saving: £42k
```

---

### Recommended Action

Example:

```
Suggested next step

Contact the client to confirm eligible R&D projects.
```

Possible actions:

* draft email
* create CRM task
* mark resolved
* dismiss signal

Completing an action may automatically resolve the Signal.

---

### Evidence and Reasoning

Each Signal explains why it was generated.

Example:

```
Evidence

• 2023 accounts show £1.2m R&D expenditure
• No R&D claim detected in prior filings
• Company recently hired 4 engineers
```

Each item links to its original source.

Signals must always be **fully traceable and cited**.

---

### Source Layer

Sources may include:

* legislation
* financial records
* emails
* documents
* filings
* public company data

Users can explore the original materials that generated the Signal.

# 14. Signal Deduplication

To prevent alert overload, similar Signals may be grouped.

Example:

Instead of multiple Signals:

```
R&D opportunities detected (5 companies)
```

---

# 15. Design Principles

The prototype should follow several principles.

### Push-first

Signals should reach advisors automatically without requiring manual searching.

### Evidence-first

Every Signal must clearly explain why it was generated.

### Low cognitive load

The interface behaves like an inbox rather than a complex dashboard.

### Action-oriented

Signals should lead directly to a next step.

### Minimal noise

Alert fatigue must be avoided through prioritisation, deduplication, and urgency scoring.

---

# 16. Success Criteria

The system is successful when:

* advisors act on Signals quickly
* opportunities are discovered earlier
* risks are identified before they escalate
* errors are prevented before reaching clients
* the system becomes a trusted intelligence layer across the firm
